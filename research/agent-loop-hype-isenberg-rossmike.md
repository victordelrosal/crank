- **Fifth research source for Crank.** The skeptic's case against autonomous loops, plus one working production loop (the "GP loop" code-review pattern).

## Core claims

1. **Loops fail on vision-heavy work because of unlogged assumptions.** A plan doc never captures the full product vision; the agent fills gaps with assumptions that usually diverge from what the human wanted.
2. **Token burn.** Loops are rational for people with unlimited tokens (Boris/Peter at Anthropic); on a $20-$100 plan they are a slot machine.
3. **Loops only work in confined, constrained processes with a fixed, defined feedback signal.** His production example: push to GitHub, Greptile reviews and scores out of 5; a "GP loop" skill reads the review, fixes, re-pushes, repeats until score >= 4/5 or 5 turns, then gives up.
4. **Empirical payload cap: the loop breaks above ~1,000 lines of code per review.** Past that the review agent cannot fully contextualize the diff and a 5/5 becomes unreachable; the fix is splitting into multiple smaller PRs.
5. **Missing mid-run human feedback.** (Isenberg) Naive loops never show the artifact to a human halfway; it is full self-driving from Miami to Charleston with no stops.
6. **Where loops DO fit:** binary-outcome work (code review against a scorer, templated SEO page generation). Where they don't (yet): anything requiring creative/product judgment. "AI can replicate sauce, it can't create sauce." "Human in the loop is the best loop" as of June 2026, with both conceding fully autonomous loops are clearly the future.

## What Crank already covers (no change needed)

Binary criteria with env-checkable preference, a cold verifier whose verdict gates the stop, max-round budget, give-up/diminishing-returns stops, hard budget ceiling in scheduled mode, downgrade transparency. Ross's "GP loop" is structurally a less general Crank: fixed scorer, threshold gate, turn cap.
