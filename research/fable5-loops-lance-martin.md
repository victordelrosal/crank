# Designing loops with Fable 5 (Lance Martin, Anthropic)

Source: X article by Lance Martin (@RLanceMartin), MTS at Anthropic. Posted 2026-06-09, 6:21 PM. ~129k views at capture time (2026-06-09 evening, Europe/Dublin). Captured because it is the first Anthropic-official voice in the June 2026 loops discourse, and it independently validates Crank's two contrarian positions: verification by an independent-context grader beats self-critique, and the grader should gate the stop.

What it adds to the Crank corpus, in order of weight:

1. **Verifier subagent > self-critique, with a reason.** "We've found that a verifier sub-agent tends to outperform self-critique with Fable 5, because grading is done in an independent context window." Cites Prithvi Rajasekaran's Anthropic engineering post on models' problems critiquing their own outputs. Their hosted product (Claude Managed Agents "Outcomes") spawns a grader subagent and "confirmed that all experimental criteria were met before allowing Claude to stop the work." Crank adopted both: the cold verifier's verdict gates DECIDE, and bet-weights is demoted to a reported confidence label.

2. **Structural beats scalar, and resilience pays.** On Parameter Golf (train the best model that fits in 16MB in under 10 minutes on 8xH100s), Fable 5 improved the pipeline ~6x more than Opus 4.7. "Fable 5 bet on larger structural changes and showed resilience (e.g., pushing through a quantization regression to its biggest win). Opus 4.7's first experiment produced a small win and nearly everything after followed the same template: adjust a scalar, measure, keep if positive." Crank's DECIDE now classifies each improvement hypothesis as structural or scalar, prefers structural when far from passing, and gives a regressing structural bet one round of follow-through.

3. **The memory progression.** On a Continual Learning Bench task (sequential questions over a SQL database, one session per question, shared memory): "effective use of memory benefits from a progression: fail (get something wrong and document), investigate (before moving on, figure out why), verify (turn the diagnosis into a checked fact), distill (turn verification into a general rule), and consult (read the rule, instead of re-deriving it)." Sonnet 4.6 exits around step 1 (a list of failure notes and open guesses it rarely consults). Opus 4.7 exits around step 3 (schema reference with uncertainty flagged; verification coverage 7 to 33 percent of questions, median run ~17 percent). Fable 5 tends to complete the progression: verification coverage up to 73 percent (22 of 30) in its strongest runs, and it distills learnings into general rules. Crank's LEARNINGS.md loop (consult at ORIENT, investigate at DECIDE, distill at handoff, verified rules only) is built directly on this.

4. **The framing.** "Rather than directly prompting and steering Fable 5, it's often better to design loops that let the model self-correct in response to environment feedback (e.g., /goal or Outcomes) and manage its own context (e.g., via memory)." /goal in Claude Code and Outcomes in CMA are named as the official primitives. Crank's environment-checkable-criteria preference follows the same logic: feedback from the environment beats feedback from an opinion.

Caveats on the capture: the numbers above (6x, 73 percent, 17 percent median) are Lance's reported small-scale experiments, not a published benchmark paper; quote them as his findings, not as ground truth. The Parameter Golf setup used CMA with 8xH100 self-hosted sandboxes and runs up to 8 hours.
